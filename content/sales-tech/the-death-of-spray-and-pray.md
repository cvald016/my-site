Let's be honest about something: most B2B sales outreach is noise. It lands in inboxes, gets skimmed in two seconds, and gets deleted without a reply. The rep who sent it adds a task to follow up in three days. Nothing changes.

The frustrating part isn't that outreach doesn't work — it's that *bad* outreach has permanently raised the bar for everyone. Buyers have been conditioned by years of generic sequences. They've developed a finely tuned allergy to anything that smells like a template.

So what actually cuts through?

## Relevance beats personalization

There's a distinction that most sales teams miss: **personalization** is about the person. **Relevance** is about why you're reaching out *right now*.

Saying "I saw you went to UCLA — go Bruins!" is personalization. It's also meaningless in a business context. Nobody buys software because a rep referenced their alma mater.

Relevance looks different. It says: "You just opened a Series B, you're hiring three sales engineers according to LinkedIn, and companies at your stage typically hit a wall with their demo infrastructure right around the time the technical win starts mattering more than the relationship win. That's exactly the problem we solve."

That message has nothing to do with the prospect's personal life. It has everything to do with their business reality right now. That's what gets replies.

## The trigger event advantage

The best reps I've worked with aren't sending outreach blindly. They're watching for signal:

- **Funding rounds** → growth mode, budget unlocking, new problems emerging
- **Job postings** → tells you what's broken and what they're trying to fix
- **Leadership changes** → new VP of Sales means new playbook, new tools, new stack evaluation
- **Product launches** → need to enable a new sales motion
- **Earnings calls / public filings** → pressure points, strategic priorities stated out loud

When you reach out within the window after a trigger event — typically the first two to four weeks — you're not interrupting. You're arriving when the timing is actually right. The message almost writes itself.

## Fewer touches, sharper edges

There's a cultural obsession in sales with sequence length. "We run a 14-touch sequence over 21 days." Great. Are touches 6 through 14 adding value, or are they just burning goodwill?

Most outreach sequences are padded to create the illusion of effort. The reality: if you haven't gotten a reply by touch three or four, it's not a volume problem. It's a message problem.

The best sequences I've seen are short and opinionated. Three to five touches, each one adding something — a different angle, a new piece of value, a specific challenge called out by name. And then a clean breakup. Something like:

> "Last note from me on this. If the timing isn't right, no hard feelings — I'll check back in a few months. If anything changes on your end, you know where to find me."

That close gets more replies than almost any middle-of-sequence touch I've written. Scarcity and directness still work.

## The technical buyer problem

This is one that doesn't get talked about enough in sales circles: **most outreach is written for the economic buyer, not the technical evaluator**.

In SaaS deals above a certain ACV, the technical buyer — the SE, the IT lead, the principal engineer — has enormous influence on the outcome. They're the ones running proof-of-concepts, stress-testing integrations, and eventually telling the economic buyer whether this is a yes or a no.

And most reps never reach them. They send LinkedIn messages to VPs and hope that message gets forwarded. It doesn't.

Reaching technical evaluators directly — with messaging that speaks to their actual concerns (implementation complexity, security posture, API quality, what happens when something breaks) — is one of the most underused levers in B2B sales. The reps who've figured this out are winning deals that others don't even realize they're losing.

## What the good ones do differently

The pattern I've seen in reps who consistently outperform isn't a magic cadence or a better tool. It's a fundamentally different mindset about what outreach is for.

Bad outreach is designed to generate activity. It's optimized for sends and opens and response rates in aggregate.

Good outreach is designed to start a specific conversation with a specific person at a specific moment. It measures success differently: not "did this get a reply?" but "did this start a conversation that was worth having?"

That shift — from activity to conversations that matter — is the whole game.
